Saving Lives
Today and Tomorrow

Managing the risk of humanitarian crises

  • Philippines, Ethiopia, Pakistan... They all have something in common.

    They have been affected by major humanitarian crises and their suffering could have been greatly reduced.


  • In the past 10 years, the number of people affected by humanitarian crises has almost doubled.


    In the same period the cost of international humanitarian aid has more than trebled.

  • Humanitarian aid is designed to provide short-term support to people affected by natural disasters or conflict.

  • ANTICIPATE, DON’T REACT

    Most crises can be predicted and while they cannot always be prevented, the suffering they cause can be greatly reduced. Preparedness can save thousands of lives and billions of dollars.

  • RISK AND AID ARE MISMATCHED

    To make a difference, we need to change how we spend money on humanitarian crises. More money and support needs to go to countries where the risk of a disaster or a conflict is highest.

  • FUNDING FOR PREPAREDNESS IS INADEQUATE

    0.5% of all international aid
    was used for prevention and preparedness between 1991 and 2010.

  • We need to change the way we work

    We need to start preventing humanitarian crises instead of just responding to them.
    This goes beyond simply fine tuning the way humanitarian organizations respond.

    Hover over icons for more information.

    Donor governments should make sure that development aid goes to the people and countries most at risk of humanitarian crises.

    Humanitarian organizations should place more emphasis on managing the risks of crises, instead of just responding to their consequences.

    Governments that fund humanitarian organizations should make crisis risk management a priority in order to prevent and mitigate future humanitarian crises.
